Sacred City of Anuradhapura

The Sacred City of Anuradhapura, located in the North Central Province of Sri Lanka, is one of the island's most ancient and revered capitals. Established in the 4th century BCE, Anuradhapura served as the center of Theravada Buddhism and Sinhalese political power for over a thousand years. The city's history is deeply interwoven with the rise of Buddhism in Sri Lanka, and it is considered one of the most important religious and archaeological sites in South Asia.

Among its many treasures, the city is home to the sacred Sri Maha Bodhi - a tree grown from a sapling of the original Bodhi tree in India, under which the Buddha attained enlightenment. The site also features grand stupas like Ruwanwelisaya, Jetavanaramaya, and Abhayagiri, as well as ancient monasteries, intricate stone carvings, and sophisticated irrigation systems. Now a UNESCO World Heritage Site, Anuradhapura remains a living spiritual center and a symbol of Sri Lanka's rich cultural and religious legacy.

Ancient City of Polonnaruwa

The Ancient City of Polonnaruwa, located in Sri Lanka's North Central Province, was the island's second capital after the decline of Anuradhapura. Flourishing between the 11th and 13th centuries CE, Polonnaruwa rose to prominence under King Vijayabahu I, and reached its golden age during the reign of King Parakramabahu I. The city became a hub of political power, Buddhist learning, and advanced urban planning, showcasing the brilliance of medieval Sinhalese architecture and hydraulic engineering.

Today, Polonnaruwa stands as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, renowned for its remarkably preserved ruins. Visitors can explore stunning monuments such as the Gal Vihara, a group of large Buddha statues carved out of a single granite rock, the Royal Palace complex, Parakrama Samudraya (an enormous man-made reservoir), and various stupas, temples, and audience halls. The layout of the city reflects meticulous planning, and its enduring structures reveal the artistic and engineering prowess of ancient Sri Lankan civilization. Polonnaruwa remains a vital link to the island's glorious past, attracting pilgrims, historians, and travelers alike.

Sacred City of Kandy

The highlight is easily the Temple of the Golden Tooth Relic. This gleaming white temple on the waterfront is part of a larger palace complex, and holds a relic sacred to all Buddhists - one of Buddha's teeth. The Tooth itself has been a symbol of Sri Lankan royalty for centuries, which explains why the temple is within a palace complex. Although the relic itself is only displayed at infrequent intervals, this is a fantastic place to come and experience the atmosphere. There's also a really interesting museum with paintings showing the story of the Tooth, and its journey from Buddha's funeral pyre to the modern city of Kandy. And as a nice bonus - Kandy has some beautiful colonial buildings too.

Golden Temple of Dambulla

The Golden Temple of Dambulla, also known as the Dambulla Cave Temple, is one of Sri Lanka's most treasured cultural and religious landmarks. Located in the Central Province, this sacred site dates back to the 1st century BCE and has served as a continuous place of worship for over 2,000 years. It is famed for its magnificent cave complex, which includes five sanctuaries carved into a massive rock over 160 meters high. These caves are adorned with intricate murals and over 150 statues of the Buddha, as well as depictions of Hindu gods and historical kings, making Dambulla the largest and best-preserved cave temple complex in the country.

Designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, the Golden Temple of Dambulla is a remarkable blend of natural and human-made artistry. The walls and ceilings of the caves are richly decorated with religious scenes and floral patterns, showcasing the evolution of Sri Lankan Buddhist art. A striking golden Buddha statue at the temple entrance adds to its spiritual and visual grandeur. The temple not only reflects the deep devotion of ancient kings and monks but also remains an active pilgrimage site for Buddhists from around the world. Its serene environment, cultural significance, and architectural splendor make it a must-visit destination in Sri Lanka.

Sigiriya

Sigiriya, also known as the Lion Rock, is a spectacular ancient fortress and palace complex located in the heart of Sri Lanka. Built in the 5th century by King Kashyapa, this towering rock rises nearly 200 meters and is surrounded by beautifully designed water gardens, moats, and ancient frescoes. Visitors can climb to the summit through a path that leads past the famous Mirror Wall and the stunning Sigiriya Damsels paintings. Recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, Sigiriya is not only an architectural and engineering marvel but also one of Sri Lanka's most visited cultural landmarks.

Yapahuwa Rock Fortress

Yapahuwa Rock Fortress, located in the North Western Province of Sri Lanka, is a magnificent ancient citadel that briefly served as the capital of the island during the 13th century. Built on a massive granite rock rising over 90 meters high, Yapahuwa was originally a military stronghold, later converted into a royal residence and temple complex by King Bhuvanekabahu I. The most iconic feature of the site is its beautifully preserved ornamental staircase, adorned with intricate stone carvings and majestic guard stones. Although much of the upper structures have faded with time, Yapahuwa still reflects the grandeur of medieval Sri Lankan architecture and artistry. Surrounded by lush greenery, this historic site offers not only cultural significance but also panoramic views and a serene atmosphere for visitors and pilgrims alike.

Sinharaja Forest Reserve

Sinharaja Forest Reserve is Sri Lanka's most significant remaining area of tropical rainforest and has been declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site due to its ecological importance. Located in the southwestern lowland wet zone of the island, Sinharaja spans over 11,000 hectares and is considered a living laboratory for biodiversity. The name "Sinharaja," meaning "Lion King," reflects the forest's majestic and untouched nature. It is one of the few places in the world where visitors can experience an unbroken expanse of rainforest that has remained relatively undisturbed for centuries.

The forest is a rich treasure trove of endemic species. Over 50% of Sri Lanka's endemic trees and more than 60% of its endemic mammals, reptiles, amphibians, and butterflies are found within Sinharaja. It is also a birdwatcher's paradise, with frequent sightings of mixed-species bird flocks, including the rare Sri Lanka blue magpie and red-faced malkoha. The dense vegetation, tangled vines, and misty trails create an enchanting atmosphere for nature lovers and researchers alike.

It acts as a vital watershed, feeding several rivers and providing clean water to surrounding areas. The forest is protected by law, and guided eco-tours are encouraged to raise awareness and promote conservation. Visiting Sinharaja offers a rare chance to connect with nature in its purest form while helping preserve one of the world's most fragile and fascinating ecosystems.

Ritigala Forest Monastery

Ritigala Forest Monastery is a fascinating and mysterious ancient monastic site nestled within the dense forest of the Ritigala Mountain Range in north-central Sri Lanka. Believed to have been established around the 1st century BCE, this monastery served as a retreat for Buddhist monks who followed strict meditation and ascetic practices. The site is spread across the forest and features a series of stone pathways, meditation platforms, rock inscriptions, and remains of ancient structures that blend harmoniously with the natural surroundings. Unlike other monasteries, Ritigala is known for its minimalistic design, reflecting the monks' disciplined lifestyle and deep connection with nature. Surrounded by myth and legend, including tales linking it to the Ramayana epic, Ritigala remains a serene and sacred space, offering both historical insight and spiritual tranquility to those who visit.

Demodara Nine Arch Bridge

The Demodara Nine Arch Bridge, located in the misty hills of Ella in Sri Lanka's central highlands, is a masterpiece of colonial-era railway construction. Built in the early 20th century during British rule, the bridge connects the Ella and Demodara railway stations on the scenic Kandy-Badulla railway line. What makes this bridge truly remarkable is that it was constructed entirely out of solid stone and bricks, without a single piece of steel - a rare feat in civil engineering. It spans 91 meters in length, rises 30 meters in height, and features nine beautifully symmetrical arches, earning it the nickname "Bridge in the Sky."


Surrounded by lush forests, tea plantations, and rolling hills, the bridge has become a beloved attraction among tourists and photographers. The sight of a bright blue train slowly curving across the massive stone arches, especially at sunrise or sunset, is nothing short of magical. A well-trodden walking path through the jungle leads visitors to the bridge, and many choose to sit along the hillside or at nearby cafes for the perfect view. The area also offers photo opportunities from below the arches, where you can appreciate the scale and craftsmanship of the structure.


Beyond its beauty, the Nine Arch Bridge also symbolizes the ingenuity and collaboration of the local people. When steel for the bridge was redirected to Britain during World War I, Sri Lankan builders and engineers reportedly used local materials and traditional techniques to complete the project. Today, the bridge stands as a testament not only to engineering brilliance but also to the cultural and historical heritage of Sri Lanka's hill country. It remains one of the most visited and admired sites in the region, drawing travelers from all over the world.
